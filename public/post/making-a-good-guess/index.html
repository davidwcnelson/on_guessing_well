Making Choices While Faced With Uncertainty

David Telson · 2020/08/07 · 4 minute read

Authors Note: I am “soft rebooting” my blog. I feel that my posts don’t communicate ideas well. I want this blog to be easy to understand. This post is a first attempt at a different approach. Let me know what you think.

This post will introduce readers to the central theme of the blog: how can we make decisions while faced with uncertainty?

I wrote this post for people who have to make decisions while faced with uncertainty (for example: anyone on any given day), but don’t yet have the tools to deal with these kinds of problems (for example: maximum expected utility). If this doesn’t describe you, that’s okay. There may still be something of use for you in this post or elsewhere in the blog.

This post doesn’t have formal pre-requisites, but if you do find yourself struggling with a concept, the fault is likely with my ability to communicate rather than your ability to learn. Let me know and I will work to clarify the ideas and update the post. Also let me know if you spot an error (we can all make mistakes, especially me).

This post will introduce you to the fundamentals of decision making under uncertainty (and in particular, making good guesses). After reading this post, you should be able to answer the following:

  • What kinds of problems can be thought of as “decision making under uncertainty”?
  • How can you enumerate your possible choices?
  • How can you enumerate the possible outcomes resulting from the possible choices?
  • How can you measure uncertainty?
  • How can you measure the value of an outcome?
  • How can you decide between choices when we are uncertain of their outcomes?

Suppose you want to watch a movie, but the only choices available to you are to rent or buy the movie. The cost of renting the movie is \(\$10.99\) and the cost of buying the movie is \(\$19.99\). Do you choose to rent or buy the movie?

Let’s break the problem down to see if we can determine which choice is better, given that we want to spend as little money as possible.

The cost of renting the movie once is less than buying the movie; however, if you want to watch the movie multiple times, then buying may be cheaper than renting. Let \(n\) be the number of times you will rewatch the movie. The cost of watching a movie \(n\) times if you rent it is \(n \times \$10.99\), whereas if you bought the movie you only pay \(\$19.99\) once.

You can find the point at which renting becomes more expensive then buying by solving an inequality. Let \(n^*\) be this point. Then \(n^* \times \$10.99\) is greater than \(\$19.99\). This means that \(n^*\) is greater than \(\frac{\$19.99}{\$10.99}\) which is approximately \(1.8\). Since \(n^*\) has to be a whole number (you can’t rent a fractio of a film) you know that \(n^*\) is \(2\). So if you watch the movie \(2\times\) or more times, then it would better to buy it rather than renting it.

The following plot provides a visual representation. The horizontal axis lists the possible times you will watch the movie, and the vertical axis displays the corresponding cost. The blue dots represent the value if you rented the movie, whereas the red dots represent the value if you bought the movie. You can see that the blue dots are greater than the red dots after you have watched the movie 1 time. Since you will watch the movie at least \(1\times\), you can boil the choice down to whether or not you will rewatch the movie. Let \(r = n - 1\) be the number of times you will rewatch the movie. Then all you need to assess is whether \(r = 0\) (in words: you will not rewatch the movie) or \(r > 0\) (in words: you will rewatch the movie). You can do this because \(n^*=2\), but in general this “reduction” won’t hold (you will learn about a more general approach soon).
